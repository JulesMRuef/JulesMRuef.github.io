Wildfires on the Beach: The Woolsey Fire

  • Over two weeks in November 2018, the Woolsey Fire burned nearly 100,000 acres in the Santa Monica Mountains north of Malibu, California. Three people were killed in the fire and over 1,600 structures were destroyed. Two other fires that ignited the same day as the Woolsey Fire caused widespread damage throughout California.

  • The Santa Ana season (Fall in Califonia), is prime time large and destructive fires. The Woolsey Fire happened in a location conducive for maximum growth; an area with little recent fire history (but history of fire going back to the 1930s) and only the beach to stop it.

General Background Info:

The Woolsey Fire and other wildfires like it increasingly threaten lives, property, and ecosystems in the American West. The escalation of fire size and duration places strain on emergency response resources and budgets, particularly as more individuals settle in the urban-wildland interface. Drought, a significant precursor to fire severity, dries out the soil and leads to vegetation mortality, providing fuel for wildfires. Preceding the Woolsey Fire was a four-year drought (2013-2016) that triggered substantial dieback of grass, shrubs, and trees throughout California

Impact of the Woolsey Fire on Vegetation

There were substantial losses of vegetation within the affected area from before the fire (2017) to after the fire (2022). It is important to recall that the inital NDVI for this region was relatively low pre-fire, thus we might expect that the magnitude of the dNDVI might not be as high as a forested region.

Vegetation slow to recover after the Woolsey Fire

  • The southern coast of California has a relatively low NDVI and the natural landscape of the Santa Monica Mountains is dominated by diverse species of woody shrubs, also known as chaparral. Wildfire is a necessary phase in the life cycle of chaparral plants. In wet years, chaparal and other native plants can regrow rapidly as compared to the large forests in the Sierra Nevada or Casacades. Despite the fact that wildfire is necessity for charral, the slow regrowth of vegetation could be in part due to the severe drought with in the state during that time period.

Despite the slow recovery, NDVI values in 2022 appear to be higher in the burned region than outside the burned region.

This phenonmenon might be caused by the following:

  • Regrowth of Vegetation: After a fire, there might be an initial decline in NDVI as vegetation is burned. However, in the subsequent years, there can be regrowth of vegetation, leading to an increase in NDVI. As mentioned above, California is home to a number of plant species that are adapted to fire, and their seeds may germinate and grow more vigorously after a fire event.

  • Succession and Recovery: Ecological succession is the process of gradual change in vegetation composition over time. After a disturbance like a fire, different plant species may dominate the area in a process known as ecological succession. As the ecosystem recovers, NDVI values may increase.

  • Climate Conditions: Climate conditions, including precipitation and temperature, can significantly influence vegetation growth. Favorable climatic conditions in the years following a fire may contribute to the recovery of vegetation and higher NDVI values.
